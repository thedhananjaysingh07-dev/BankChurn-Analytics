2. HYPOTHESIS SCORECARD
----------------------------------------------------------------
  H1  Older customers churn less (switching cost theory)
      STATUS   : REJECTED
      EVIDENCE : <30 churns least (7.52%). 46-60 churns most
                 (51.12%) — more than double the overall avg.
                 60+ sits at 24.78%, moderate.
      TAKEAWAY : Loyalty does not rise linearly with age.
                 Financially confident, high-earning mid-life
                 customers are the least loyal, not the most.

  H2  Germany churns more due to stronger local competition
      STATUS   : CONFIRMED
      EVIDENCE : Germany 32.44% vs France 16.15% / Spain 16.67%
                 — almost exactly double. Matches external PwC
                 benchmark of 32.4% for Germany.
      TAKEAWAY : Country-specific problem, not a product-wide
                 issue. Retention strategy must be localized.

  H3  Single/high-product customers with high balance are a
      hidden high-risk segment
      STATUS   : CONFIRMED (via CLV analysis)
      EVIDENCE : High CLV churn 24.12%, Premium CLV churn
                 24.20% — both above the 20.37% overall average,
                 and both hold the largest average balances
                 among churned customers (€117.5K and €138K).
      TAKEAWAY : The bank disproportionately loses its most
                 profitable customers, not its average ones.

  H4  Inactive members may be dormant savers, not disengaged
      STATUS   : PARTIALLY CONFIRMED
      EVIDENCE : Inactive members churn at 26.85% vs Active at
                 14.27% — engagement does correlate with churn.
                 BUT Zero-balance customers churn LEAST of all
                 balance segments (13.82%), supporting the
                 "dormant safety-net account" theory.
      TAKEAWAY : Two different inactive populations exist —
                 genuinely disengaged leavers, and quiet
                 zero-balance holders who are not at risk.

  H5  Female customers churn more (service-quality sensitivity)
      STATUS   : CONFIRMED
      EVIDENCE : Female churn 25.07% vs Male 16.46% — a 52%
                 relative gap. Female churn pulls the overall
                 average upward; male churn sits near benchmark.
      TAKEAWAY : Retention messaging / service review should be
                 segmented by gender, not generic.

  H6  New customers churn more due to "honeymoon" promotional
      behaviour
      STATUS   : WEAKLY SUPPORTED
      EVIDENCE : Tenure groups show little spread — New 21.15%,
                 Mid 20.64%, Long 19.51%. Direction is correct
                 (newer = higher) but the magnitude is small.
      TAKEAWAY : Promotional churn may exist but is not a major
                 standalone driver in this dataset — likely
                 entangled with the Product-count effect below.


3. NON-OBVIOUS / SURPRISING INSIGHTS
----------------------------------------------------------------
  a) U-SHAPED PRODUCT CHURN (most important secondary finding)
     1 product  : 27.71% churn
     2 products : 7.58%  churn  ← safest segment in dataset
     3 products : 82.71% churn  ← near-catastrophic
     4 products : 100.00% churn ← total loss, every customer
     Reading    : 2 products = healthy engagement. 3-4 products
                  likely reflects aggressive cross-selling that
                  damages trust rather than building loyalty.

  b) CREDIT SCORE IS NOT A CHURN DRIVER
     High 20.18% | Medium 19.64% | Low 22.15% — a spread of
     only 2.5 points. Contradicts the common banking assumption
     that creditworthiness predicts loyalty.

  c) TENURE GROUP IS NOT A STRONG CHURN DRIVER
     New 21.15% | Mid 20.64% | Long 19.51% — flat. Loyalty does
     not meaningfully build over time in this dataset.

  d) CREDIT CARD OWNERSHIP HAS NO EFFECT
     Has card 20.18% vs No card 20.81% — statistically
     indistinguishable. A commonly assumed "stickiness" feature
     turns out to be irrelevant.

  e) ZERO-BALANCE CUSTOMERS ARE THE MOST LOYAL SEGMENT
     13.82% churn, lowest of any Balance segment — counter-
     intuitive, supports dormant-account / safety-net behaviour
     rather than disengagement.

5. KEY TERMINOLOGY (for consistent use in the paper)
----------------------------------------------------------------
  Topline       = Revenue (top line of P&L statement)
  Bottom line   = Net profit
  CLV           = Customer Lifetime Value (weighted: Balance
                  50% + Salary 30% + Tenure 20%)
  Switching cost = Friction (paperwork, habit, perceived risk)
                  that discourages a customer from leaving

7. OPEN QUESTIONS / LIMITATIONS TO MENTION IN PAPER
----------------------------------------------------------------
  • Dataset lacks transaction-level / call-centre / NPS data,
    so root causes (e.g. why Germany churns more) are inferred,
    not directly observed.
  • Low Balance segment churn (34.67%) is based on a small
    sample (75 customers) — flagged as low-confidence.
  • Product identity (what products 1-4 actually are) is
    assumed from typical European retail banking patterns,
    not confirmed by the dataset itself.
